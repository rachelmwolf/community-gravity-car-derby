# Marketing Plan

## Purpose

This document defines how the Community Gravity Car Derby should be presented to the public and how outreach should be prioritized for year one.

## Core Positioning

The event should be marketed as:

- community-first
- playful and welcoming
- build-at-home, race-together
- scouting-adjacent in spirit, but not scout-branded in appearance

## Main Goal

Sell up to `60` car entries without making the event feel exclusive or insider-focused.

## Secondary Goals

- attract new families and community members who would enjoy a build-and-race event
- create positive local awareness around the event
- make it easy for interested families to find scouting resources without making scouting the main message
- support future repeatability by preserving message templates and outreach timing

## Primary Audiences

### Best-Fit Audiences

- non-scout community families
- former scout families
- neighborhood families
- church families and other community groups
- grandparents or other adults looking for a project with kids
- adults who want to participate on their own or with friends

### Welcome But Not Primary Target

- current scout families

## Offer

- `$35` per ticket
- each ticket equals one car entry
- each ticket includes one kit with wood block, axles, and wheels
- cars are built at home
- cars may be embellished within the event rules
- participants may purchase more than one entry

## Message Hierarchy

Use these in this order.

### 1. What It Is

`A fun community race where you build a gravity car at home and race it together.`

### 2. Who It Is For

`Families, grandparents, friends, and adults are all welcome.`

### 3. What You Get

`Each ticket includes one car kit and one race entry.`

### 4. What Makes It Appealing

- creative
- hands-on
- local
- welcoming
- memorable

### 5. What Makes It Easy

- build at home
- simple starting kit included
- resources available for help

## Suggested Tagline Options

- `Build at home. Race together.`
- `Build a car. Bring your people. Race for fun.`
- `A playful community race for builders of all kinds.`

## Recommended Year-One Channel Mix

Given the budget and available channels, prioritize:

### 1. Facebook

Use for:

- launch post
- reminder posts
- shared community-group posts
- neighborhood and parent-group visibility

### 2. Peachjar / School Distribution

Use for:

- family-friendly reach
- broad awareness outside scouting circles

### 3. Flyers And Posters

Use for:

- storefront partners
- community boards
- church/community locations

### 4. Storefront Partners

Use for:

- direct local visibility
- conversational selling
- handout pickup

### 5. Email

Use only where you already have a legitimate list or partner channel.

## Budget Guidance

Keep year-one paid spend modest.

Recommended use of a `$100` budget:

- printing flyers and posters
- one small paid social boost only if organic reach is weak

Do not spread a small budget across too many experiments.

## Recommended Tone Rules

- do not sound like a formal race league
- do not sound like a scout-only event
- do not over-explain the rules in awareness marketing
- do sound welcoming, local, and easy to join
- do mention that adults and nontraditional family groups are welcome

## Conversion Path

Every public post or flyer should move people toward one next step:

- buy a ticket
- learn what is included
- learn where to get help building

If a piece does not move people toward one of those, it is probably too vague.

## Suggested Timeline Structure

### Launch Window

- announce the event
- publish the ticket link
- explain what a ticket includes

### Build Window

- remind people to buy before capacity fills
- share resources and build help
- highlight that all kinds of families and adults are welcome

### Final Push

- remind people of the deadline
- remind them to finish their cars
- remind them what to bring and when to arrive

## What To Avoid

- making scouting the lead headline
- making the event sound like it is only for children currently in cub scouts
- overcrowding marketing with too many details
- relying on email as the primary acquisition channel if the list is weak

## Year-One Recommendation

For year one, optimize for clarity and warmth over cleverness:

- clear event name
- simple offer
- broad welcome
- repeated reminders
- easy registration path
